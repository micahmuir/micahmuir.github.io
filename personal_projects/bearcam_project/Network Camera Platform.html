Network Camera Platform

I created a raspberry pi based system to acquire and transmit a variety of depth and image data over a network, primarily for interactive installation usage, but also as a test and learning platform. The above video shows the real-time ToF output visualized and processed in TouchDesigner.

Background and Motivation

I like cameras, as a tool for both measurement and self expression. I wanted a reliable camera that wasn’t my 20 year old DSLR that I could easily rig and position and patch into existing electronic projects. At the same time, I also wanted to gain experience using board-level cameras and custom processing programs to satisfy my own curiosity.

I cobbled together a Pi 5, a 12V ringlight, some mosfets, a 16 MP IMX519 and a 5MP IMX296 camera module to get to work. A couple of python scripts later, I had a timelapse camera that could save and stitch them back together with OpenCV.

I learned a lot here about OpenCV integration on Raspberry Pis. This was all well and good, but the above contraption would get a loose wire if you looked at it funny. This, combined with a previous project where I took the raw frame depth buffer from ToF camera and transmitted as UDP packets had me realize I could achieve a low cost RGBD camera specifically suited for my areas of interest.

After testing a dual ToF configuration (too much multipath interference), I settled on a configuration of one ToF camera and one RGB camera per head. When I was getting into this project initially, I ordered a global shutter and a rolling shutter camera module to compare performance in different applications.


Because these new Pi’s have so much computing power, I wanted to have these camera systems be expandable with additional modules and sensors. I thought it would be fun to design a series of modular “ears” each head could be paired with to change its capabilities down the road. Maybe one has extra radars, or one actually has audio microphones onboard. Who knows what might be possible?

I sketched out a rough shell design, and floated the components in my CAD program to place them within the housing.

The outer shell was fun to model. I revolved an ellipse to form the bulk of the head, and surfaced the cheeks, eyes, and nose area until it looked right.

I made sure to drop a 1/4-20 tap hole on the bottom for tripod mounting, and sliced the shell to make it feasibly printable on my FDM printer.

The below diagram shows the basic layout of the device. On the very back is the voltage regulator - this downsteps 12-24V to 5V at 10 amps. I’ll put a fuse in, and then have a bunch of extra power for anything I should need.

I made use of the Pi 5’s pcie slot as well - each head has a 512 gb NVME drive for snappy start and data read/write. I plan to collect large point clouds and image sets with these heads, so fast writing and large storage is essential.

I left extra space around nose/mouth of each head for future sensors. Right now in the rolling shutter head, there’s an A111 Acconeer mmwave radar. It’s hooked the SPI interface on the pi, and I plan to use the precise tracking ability in future installations.

Below shows the assembled image packets on a Touchdesigner application the head is patched into. After some testing, I found a compression setting that worked well enough for a 320x240 image at 30fps from both cameras, but only monochrome.

From there, I used a small GLSL shader to remap the depth image into an XYZ point cloud from the aspect ratio and diagonal FoVs of the ToF camera. Like my interactive mat project, I used instancing and textures to leverage my graphics card for efficient rendering.
