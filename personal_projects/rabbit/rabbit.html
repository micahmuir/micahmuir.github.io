RABBIT

Motivation

A year and a half ago, I got an idea for a project where we’d have two people on a darkened stage, moving together in a call-response fashion. When moving synchronously, beams of light flared out across space to connect them, but only when they mirrored each other within a certain threshold. Maybe their opposing x axis velocities? How would I measure this? I didn’t know how I was going to achieve this exactly, but I knew it wasn’t impossible. Through my job at Sherpa, I began to gain exposure to very low level time-of-flight integrations. I knew I ultimately wanted this environment not to rely on visible light, so infrared measurement seemed like the obvious option.

Requirements

This project was borne out of need for a low cost, high-ish performance network camera solution for my interactive spatial projects. I needed a generalized method and hardware to locate a human in 3D space and perform pose estimation

It needed to do the following:

  • Provide real-time depth imaging alongside simultaneous “regular” imaging
  • Be relatively small
  • Send imaging data across CAT5/6 network cables as fast as I possible can.
  • Leverage a much more powerful central computer to perform GPU calculation

Software Build

Enter Touchdesigner. Although intended as a tool for live entertainment and visuals, I quickly discovered its power as a sort of connective “glue” between the hardware nodes for this project.

Yes, I can use touchdesigner to make pretty pictures, but I can ALSO use it to accept high volumes of network data. I couldn’t afford a proper GiGe camera, but I had enough hubris to think I could spoof one for low-ish resolutions. As 180x240 pixels times two cameras, that wasn’t that much data to send overall.

If I could send a panoramic depth image with 32bit float values to a touchdesigner script, then I could leverage my GPU to do all sorts of interesting things with the data.

Future Improvements
